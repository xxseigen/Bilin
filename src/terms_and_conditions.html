Food Donations

  1. Donor and Recipient

    2. When donating food to charitable organizations and community pantries (“Recipients”), the Donor should make sure that the food is safe, wholesome, and of good quality. It is highly recommended that the Bilin Food Donation Guidelines be followed.

    If the Recipient accepts the donation, the Recipient agrees to hold the Donor harmless and to indemnify the Donor for any civil damages including, but not limited to, attorney’s fees, claims, suits, and judgments of any kind arising from the use of food donated by the Donor.

    The Recipient and Donor shall mutually agree on a schedule for food donation pick up or distribution.

  2. Unacceptable Food Donations

    3. Certain foods are not suitable for donation because of safety concerns. These foods include:

    • Perishable foods past a “use by” or “consume by” date, unless frozen.
    • Foods with opened, damaged, or compromised packaging
    • Canned foods that are swollen, heavily dented, or corroded
    • Packaged foods with missing or incomplete source/manufacturer labels
    • Any food that has been contaminated by insects, rodents, or chemicals
    • Foods that have been exposed to fire, flooding, excessive heat, smoke, radiation, other environmental contamination, or prolonged storage must not be donated for consumption by a consumer. Foods exposed to the listed conditions may be sold or donated to a licensed food salvage.
    • Foods previously served to consumers
    • Foods prepared, cooked, cooled, or reheated at home
    • High-risk foods obtained from premises without a valid Food Handling Permit
    • Meat and poultry which have not been inspected
    • Donated fish products not caught under a commercial license
    • Eggs that are leaking, cracked, or visibly dirty
  3. Acceptable Food Donations
    • The donation of commercially canned, boxed, and otherwise packaged foods is encouraged.
    • Food donations may include fresh produce, including home-grown gruits and vegetables. Fresh produce should be protected from contamination.
    • Licensed food establishments are encouraged to donate surplus foods. Because licensed food establishments have commercial-grade equipment, these may safely include foods that have gone through typical multiple food preparation steps and are handled with the same consideration for safety as the food sold to customers.
    • Perishable goods past the original manufacturer's “sell by” (or “best if used by”) date are suitable for donation, but not foods past a “use by” (or “consume before”) date. Other items not suitable for donation include foods that have been temperature abused; foods that have been served to a customer's table; spoiled foods; foods exposed to potential contamination by severe package damage; and sharply dented or rusty cans.
    • Food processors are encouraged to donate foods that may not meet their specifications for reasons that do not affect food safety, such as package printing errors. Each food package may lack the complete labeling required for commercial distribution, as long as the charitable distributing organization is given enough information to mark each container with the common name of the food. At least one accurate, complete label must be provided with each master carton.
  4. Liability for Damages from Donated Food

    5. In accordance with Republic Act No. 9803 Section 5, “a person, whether natural or juridical, shall not be subject to civil or criminal liability arising from the nature, age, packaging, or condition of apparently wholesome food that a person donates in good faith for charitable purposes.
